01 · The problem

Sleep technology doesn't have a data problem. It has an action problem.

In 2025, 1 in 3 adults sleep poorly, not because they lack tracking devices, but because those devices never work together, never act on what they know, and never help the user understand what to change.

{{ms.value}}

{{ms.body}}

{{ms.source}}
Design opportunity

How might we move users from passive sleep tracking to active, personalized sleep improvement, without adding complexity, friction, or cognitive load?

Before NURA: too much data, no action

Leila owns a smartwatch, sleep app, thermostat, and white noise machine. Every device collects data. None work together. Every morning she sees charts, but no answer to why she slept poorly. Technology has become another source of stress.

The vision

One intelligence layer. Every device in the bedroom, finally talking.

NURA doesn't add another gadget. It sits in the middle of the ones you already own, senses what they see, and acts as a single system while you sleep.

Biometrics flow in from every device. NURA reads them together and adjusts the room, in real time, without a single tap.

On-device processing

  • Under 200ms latency, instant response to biometric changes
  • Works with no internet, even during outages
  • Biometric data never leaves the device

Cloud alternatives

  • 2 to 5 second latency, too slow for real-time adaptation
  • Network-dependent, fails without Wi-Fi
  • Sensitive health data transmitted off-device
06 · Design language

One system, two emotional states: morning clarity and nighttime calm.

Same components, spacing, and interaction patterns, with a complete emotional shift between modes.

Night mode · sleep context
  • Deep navy gradient reduces eye strain and signals rest
  • Frosted blue glass cards, soft edges, low cognitive demand
  • Aqua accents for insights and AI action indicators
  • Oversized tap targets for eyes-half-closed, bedside use
  • Soft easing on every animation, nothing abrupt at 3 AM
Day mode · review context
  • Soft cream gradient for morning energy and warmth
  • Light frosted cards, airy and data-forward for scanning
  • Warm orange accents matched to morning review energy
  • Simplified to 3 primary metrics after data-overload testing
  • Insight callouts surface the week's most significant pattern
Component
Design decision and rationale
8px spacing grid
Every layout follows 8, 16, 24, 32px increments. It creates rhythm and predictability, which matters most in a nighttime UI where cognitive load has to stay minimal.
Rounded glass cards
Frosted glass surfaces feel calm and soft. The same treatment carries across control buttons, insight graphs, mood check cards, and weekly charts.
Adaptation animations
When NURA adjusts something, the control card animates softly, a slow eased bar fill. Abrupt motion would be jarring at 3 AM.
Accessibility layer
Reduced-motion option, large tap targets, high contrast in both themes, and a VoiceOver-friendly focus order. Built for users like Sarah who rely on accessibility features.

Critical testing insight

Users told during onboarding that NURA needed 7 nights to learn were far more tolerant of early imperfect recommendations. Users who weren't told read a wrong recommendation on night 3 as product failure. Onboarding does not just set up the product, it sets up the entire trust relationship with the AI.

14 · Success metrics and KPIs

Measured across three layers: sleep outcome, product health, and business.

Each metric traces back to a design decision. Override rate measures trust: if users keep undoing NURA, the explainability layer failed. Latency is why the AI runs on-device.

Validation plan

A 90-day pilot with 50 users, recruited through sleep-disorder communities, tests these metrics before any OEM partnership. Participants receive Apple Watches and smart-bed integration in exchange for feedback and consent. Success is 70% of participants showing measurable sleep improvement by week four.
