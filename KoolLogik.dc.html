Case study, Tundra & KoolLOGIK

Redesigning safety-critical HMI for commercial refrigeration.

I led end-to-end research and redesign of a high-stakes industrial interface for Thermo-Kool walk-in coolers and freezers. I replaced a memorization-dependent process with a guided setup, designed a tiered alarm system that makes urgency impossible to miss, and laid the foundation for a connected monitoring platform, KoolLOGIK.

Industrial controls built for experts, used by everyone.

04 · Setup flow

From 10 memorized steps to a guided 5-screen setup.

The legacy install was a non-linear menu navigated from memory. I rebuilt it as a linear, progressive wizard that scales from a single-room cooler to an 8-room commercial complex.

Before · 10 steps
  • Power on
  • Manual configuration from memory
  • Consult printed manual off-site
  • Trial and error
  • Complete, with no validation
After · 4 guided steps
  • Power on
  • Guided wizard with visible progress
  • Physical door identification and validation
  • Ready, validated at every step
Step 1 · Welcome and time estimate

Orient the technician immediately.

System context. States exactly what product is being configured, with no ambiguity for multi-brand technicians.
Time estimate. "Est. time 3 to 5 min" sets expectations upfront and reduces mid-flow abandonment.
Single action. One primary CTA. No menus, no settings. Expert mode and full manual live behind a QR code.
Step 2 · Configuration model

Start from a template, not a blank slate.

Five pre-built templates cover 90% of real Thermo-Kool installations. Custom configuration is still available for non-standard setups, just not the default path.

Step 3 · Room count that scales

The same screen, from 1 room to 8.

A stepper with a live 3D visualization makes the abstract concrete. The identical interaction handles a single convenience-store cooler and an 8-room kitchen complex, with no separate UI.

1 room, the most common small install.

8 rooms, maximum complexity, same screen.

Step 4 · Name your rooms

Real names make every later screen human.

Technicians name each room before door assignment, so the rest of the flow reads "Back Freezer" and "Walk-In Cooler" instead of abstract labels like "Zone 1". Small change, large drop in downstream confusion.

Step 5 · Physical door identification

Open the door. It turns green.

The most innovative step. Instead of specifying which sensor maps to which door, the technician physically opens each door and the indicator turns green in real time, making identification a physical, unambiguous act.

✓ {{b}}
Step 6 · Validation

Verify, then confirm.

Automated sensor verification runs in the background with per-room status, so technicians see exactly what passed. A clear success state confirms what was configured, then hands off to the live KoolLOGIK dashboard.

05 · Dashboard

Organized by operational priority, not by menu.

Once setup is done, technicians and kitchen managers use KoolLOGIK daily. The dashboard prioritizes instant status recognition over information density, because in a commercial kitchen you need answers in seconds, not minutes.

06 · Alarm hierarchy

Urgency you recognize before you read a word.

The legacy interface showed every alarm identically. A compressor failure and a door-ajar notice were visually indistinguishable. I rebuilt alarms as a four-tier hierarchy reinforced through color, border weight, typography, iconography, and sound at once.

{{t.tag}}
{{t.name}}

{{t.body}}

{{ex}}

Warning state

An amber border and a chime flag a door left open or a rising temperature. Attention is needed, but it is not yet an emergency.

Critical state

The whole room card turns red, a critical badge appears, and the temperature is displayed large, the data that matters most when food safety is at risk.

Panic alarm

A manual emergency trigger takes over the full screen with a countdown and a single "Clear Panic" action. No navigation is possible until it is acknowledged.

Design rationale, color language

The system uses a traffic-light convention, blue for information, amber for caution, red for danger, so technicians interpret it correctly on first contact with no training. Color is never the sole signal: each tier also carries a distinct border weight, type scale, icon, and sound, for accessibility in loud, bright kitchens.

08 · Usability testing and iteration

Two rounds. The setup flow was harder than expected.

The sharpest insights came from the door identification step and the alarm hierarchy, the two places where the redesign departed most boldly from the legacy system.

Critical testing insight

The door identification step was the most innovative and the most confusing in round one. The concept was right; the language was wrong. Switching from sensor terminology to a direct instruction, "open Door 1 to identify it", resolved all confusion in round two. Clever UX still needs clear language to work.
